## What is the Tree of Life?

The Tree of Life is the central symbol of the Qabalah — a map of existence used in the Western Hermetic and Mystical traditions. It depicts the structure of creation as a series of ten interconnected spheres, known as Sephiroth, linked by twenty-two Paths.

In this tradition, the Tree is understood as a map of consciousness, a symbolic framework for exploring the cosmos and the self. Each sphere and path carries rich symbolic meaning that can be studied, meditated upon, and applied to understanding one's own nature and life.

---

## The ten Sephiroth

The ten spheres of the Tree are arranged on three pillars — the Pillar of Mercy, the Pillar of Severity, and the Middle Pillar of Balance. From the highest to the lowest, they are commonly described as:

- Kether — Crown, pure being, the source
- Chokmah — Wisdom, dynamic force, the father principle
- Binah — Understanding, receptive form, the mother principle
- Chesed — Mercy, expansion, grace
- Geburah — Strength, severity, discipline
- Tiphareth — Beauty, harmony, the heart of the Tree
- Netzach — Victory, feeling, natural force
- Hod — Splendour, intellect, communication
- Yesod — Foundation, the astral, the unconscious
- Malkuth — Kingdom, the earth, physical manifestation

---

## The twenty-two Paths

The twenty-two Paths connecting the Sephiroth correspond to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet and the twenty-two Major Arcana of the Tarot. Each Path represents a quality of consciousness or a stage of inner development.

Studying the Paths is considered a natural progression from studying the Sephiroth — moving from understanding the spheres of being to understanding the transitions and forces between them.

---

## The Tree as a living map

In Stoic Qabalah, the Tree of Life is explored not as a static diagram but as a living symbolic system. Students work through each sphere and path in depth — using PDF handouts, visualisations, reflective exercises, and astrological correspondence — to understand how these symbols relate to their own inner life and outer experience.

The Tree is used as a tool for self-knowledge, balance, and spiritual study. It is not a system of prediction or deterministic fate.
