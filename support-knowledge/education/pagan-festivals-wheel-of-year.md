## What is the Wheel of the Year?

The Wheel of the Year is a symbolic calendar marking eight key points in the solar cycle. It tracks the movement of the Sun through the year — solstices, equinoxes, and the four cross-quarter days between them.

In the Western esoteric and pagan traditions, these eight festivals are understood as thresholds — moments when the quality of natural energy shifts, and when reflection, ritual, and intention carry particular resonance.

In Stoic Qabalah, the Wheel of the Year is explored through the 12 Shining Lanterns work, which follows the Sun through each of the twelve zodiac signs as it traces the complete solar cycle.

---

## The eight festivals

**Imbolc** — approximately 1–2 February
Marks the first stirrings of spring. Traditionally associated with purification, new beginnings, and the return of light. Linked to the midpoint between the Winter Solstice and Spring Equinox.

**Spring Equinox (Ostara)** — approximately 20–21 March
Day and night are equal. Traditionally associated with balance, renewal, and the beginning of the active half of the year. The Sun enters Aries — the first zodiac sign — at this point.

**Beltane** — approximately 1 May
Marks the full arrival of spring and the beginning of summer. Traditionally associated with vitality, fertility, creative force, and the peak of growing energy.

**Summer Solstice (Litha / Midsummer)** — approximately 20–21 June
The longest day. Traditionally associated with the height of solar power, abundance, and the fullness of outward expression. The Sun enters Cancer.

**Lammas (Lughnasadh)** — approximately 1 August
Marks the first harvest. Traditionally associated with gratitude, the beginning of the inward turn, and the acknowledgement of what has been grown and gathered.

**Autumn Equinox (Mabon)** — approximately 22–23 September
Day and night are equal again. Traditionally associated with balance, reflection, and the second harvest. The Sun enters Libra. The inward half of the year begins.

**Samhain** — approximately 31 October – 1 November
Marks the end of the harvest and the beginning of the dark half of the year. Traditionally associated with the ancestors, the thinning of the veil between worlds, and deep reflection on endings and transformation. One of the most significant festivals in many pagan traditions.

**Winter Solstice (Yule)** — approximately 21–22 December
The longest night. Traditionally associated with the return of the light, the rebirth of the solar principle, and the promise of renewal within darkness. The Sun enters Capricorn.

## The Wheel of the Year and astrology

The eight festivals align with key astrological moments — the solstices and equinoxes mark the Sun's entry into the four Cardinal signs (Aries, Cancer, Libra, Capricorn), while the cross-quarter days fall at the midpoints of the Fixed signs (Taurus, Leo, Scorpio, Aquarius).

This connection between the seasonal festivals and the zodiac reflects the deep roots of astrology in the observation of natural cycles.

---

## Samhain — a note

Samhain (pronounced roughly SAH-win) is often considered the most liminal of the eight festivals — a threshold between the old year and the new, between the outer world and the inner. In Stoic Qabalah, it may be explored as a time for ancestral reflection, releasing what no longer serves, and entering the deep inner work of the dark season.
